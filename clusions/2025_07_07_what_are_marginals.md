# What are ‘Marginals’? 

Marginals are a type of writing, written, to give voice to one’s [**crickets**](../bat[hh]ouse/2025_07_19_roses.md); wherein, said ‘crickets’ are what appear from the non-understanding of the word; be it written or spoken. 

>Usually, it is those moments when we are met with a ‘**cognitive stare**’—of sorts—in that, it is that which crumbles when a stare is encumbered in the wake of not knowing. 

This is to say; we can read, and we can watch, and we can listen, but rarely do we know what we are thinking? Truly? Therein lies the **cognitive itch**. 

>Although, this definition may use the plural “we”, here “we” is not factually plural; rather, it principlises two versions of the same narrative, where “we” is either: “I with you”, or “I without you”. And so, “we” is not bound by contract, and “you” may exit the itch. 

To break it down, into bites, we could say: 

>A **marginal** is an individual comment, or fragment (or, if you allow, a ‘cognitive itch’), written in the margins of thought. 

>**Marginals** are a collection of these individual pieces, gathered as a set of ‘margins of thought’. 

>[**Marginalia**](../marginalia) are the little notes, scribbles, or comments collectively written in the margins of a text.

In an attempt to unconfuse the confused, let me make note that: marginals are not in fact marginalia, though they may share similar forms: **marginals do not require a parent text**; they emerge at the edge of understanding, be it through: reading, thinking, or witnessing. 

Take, for example, my introductive commentary on Nabokov’s ‘Lolita’: 

>*”[This is a (singular—much ado) thought I hold on the book, ‘Lolita’, which I have not graced myself to read; while I am not discussing the text, I am however, be it in clear vision and hearing, writing in response to its peculiar reception.](../marginalia/2025_07_05_nabokov_lotita.md)”*

Let us hone in on “much ado”—this is a marginal; in that it is a gesture that ‘winks’ to Shakespeare’s ‘Much Ado About Nothing’, and not ‘Lolita’ itself. And so, we can conclude that it was not an act of writing on the written word. If, I had commented in close proximity to the source material, then one could argue that I had written a marginalia. Here, that is not the case. 

*So, while marginals may become marginalia, not all marginalia are marginals. **It is a question born of textual proximity and origin***; in other words, we must wonder whether the comment is rooted in the source or simply passing through its influence ([and, we must have observed (by now) that all that is written is burdened, to some degree, by influence: known and unknown](../bat[hh]ouse/2025_07_04_margins.md)). 

While I am, to much friction, attempting to be explicit in definition for the sake of clarity, allow me to propose: 

>Marginals are written to bend and maybe, if possible, break the rules of language; *it is an act of allowing thought to flow through bent syntax with the permission to take rest in the breath-wells homed in punctuation (—therein lies a half truth)*. 

And, if we were to get slightly poetic, we might say: **a marginal is the act of itching that which stares in a bathhouse of crickets.**
