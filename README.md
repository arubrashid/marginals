> # FORWORT.   
>
> In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. I, Arub, daughter of Adil Rashid and Shagufta Tausf Rashid, born to the care of my grandparents Abdur Rashid and Hamida Begum Rashid, and Taj Muhammad and Soraya Begum, and held accountable to the generations of shoulders upon which I herein stand, have composed these writings for reasons unbeknownst to myself, hoping they may benefit the sincere seeker of knowledge. May Allah forgive my errors and guide my pen toward truth.  
>
> ——
>
> # PREFACE. 
>
> When one starts a writing such as this, they must hold their discomfit tight, lest they fall when faced with their reflection: the reflection of ‘I.’
> 
> Yet, face I must. 
> 
> For how else does one suppose I explain marginals?  
> 
> Do you see? The ‘I’ haunts this writing already, and we are only forty *characters* in. There is much to despair indeed. And with such despair comes the ever-looming, maudlin fate, as feared by Rodrigo, the protagonist of Clarice Lispector’s *The Hour of the Star*.
> 
> The Rodrigo himself is a funhouse of reflections. He becomes a vehicle whereby the reader reflects into Macabéa, out into Clarice herself, and sideways into his immediate surroundings. Perhaps, Rodrigo was Clarice’s way of holding her discomfort. 
> 
> *The Hour of the Star*, in its utter mess, proposes that there is an ever-looming fate braided into one’s hold for life. And what is life but uncomfortable? Therefore, should discomfort be a ledge, our fate would be a guaranteed slip, sneaking its way into our bones and rattling us to fall.
> 
> That being said, it should not be preposterous for me to lay out some cushions in an effort to preserve my bones from a slip-fated fall. 
> 
> For me, cushions are stitched and lain through art. In this instance, said art is presented through the act of writing. As to write is to mould cushions of hope, yet such laying is one of consequence. And these consequences rippled across both body and mind. Here one encounters a deep personal refusal: an active friction against *je pense, donc je suis*, and its later Latin counterpart *cogito, ergo sum* as put forth by René Descartes in his *Mediations*. 
> 
> Such a refusal is slippery, while there is much to gain from his mediations, I cannot help but stumble across *cogito, ergo sum*. Perhaps it is the Muslim in me. 
> 
> Let us take the human, and pull it from pure anthropocentrism and put forth a thought experiment: should death *knock* on man and his body buried, be it in whole or through cremated remains, one might wonder: where have his thoughts gone? And do his thoughts cease on death?
> 
> The latter of the two questions can be understood through René’s conclusion that the “body can very easily perish, but that the mind is of its nature immortal.” This he built from his earlier statement that “we can understand no body except as divisible, but on the other hand no mind except as indivisible: for we cannot conceive a half of any mind, as we can with any body, however small it is.”
> 
> To which I wonder: should a man lose a leg, would his mind then not walk limp? For what guarantees the definition of whole? Let us propose there is another man who was born without a leg, and would this not make his body whole by his relative perspective? Then, by this argument, his mind would remain whole too, for he has never known what it means to walk with two legs. A stance the first man would not be able to hold. 
> 
> Would such an inquiry not suppose that the mind, like the body, is divisible? Whereby, such a division is of one’s relative experiences and should not be generalised? Take, for example, the earth: it is large in comparison to the moon, but small when compared to the sun. Man is like this too. The two men when generalised would be labelled as devised, yet should you ask the second man, you would find an answer at generalisable odds.
> 
> This is to say, the body and mind cannot be arbitrarily pulled apart. Though an attempt was made in René’s argument: “the human body, insofar as it differs from the rest of bodies, is constituted purely by a certain arrangement of parts and by other accidents of the same kind; whereas the mind is not composed of any accidents in this way, but is a pure substance. For even if all its accidents were to change, for instance, if it understood, or wished, or perceived via the senses a different set of things, it would still be the same mind. On the other hand, the human body becomes something different from the very fact that the shape of some of its parts is changed. From this it follows that the body can very easily perish, but that the mind is of its nature immortal.” 
> 
> In order to respond to this argument, we must first return to the man who has passed from earlier and invoke Antoine Lavoisier, who gave us the law of the conservation of mass. While some may contest my invocation of Antoine in this manner, and suggest that it is edged with ridiculousness, arguing that the universe is not a closed system, I can only respond as such: do you have it in writing?
> 
> In its most simplistic sense, the law recalls that nature merely transforms itself, which is also true of the body, no? So let us look to the body, one that has passed, and follow its journey of decay. 
> 
> Though we must note that decay is a complex process. Therefore, in an effort to further simplify we may focus our attention on decay by way of ions leached from a body.  
> 
> Once leached, and leaching, ions may travel and feed to plant. Then that which was once human would now be a sunflower? Should a bee graze the pollen and pass it to a rose? A rose, then eaten by a beaver? And what if the beaver is *knocked* by water, would the once-human ions now be a running stream? Algae to fish to vapour? And should vapour be sky-held, would our past ions rest in a cloud? What of a cloud that galaxy escapes, its *gravity in-felt*—would the ions of the once-man give life to a star? 
> 
> Would this then not make one question the arbitrary split of mind and body? For the body never truly ceases in its existence, for conservation is one of transformation, not of destruction nor creation. I suppose by *accidents*, René might also have been invoking the law of the conservation of mass? Though he does make it clear the “body can very easily perish,” which gives rise to conflicting statements in his *Meditations*. 
> 
> Let us take a closer look at the body: the body is said to be approximately sixty percent water. This is a point of note. While skirting speculation, I will point to Lev Landau whose experiments demonstrated that water exhibits a persistence of structure in a flow. Therein, he noted that coherent patterns were able to endure, however briefly, in the constant rearrangement of molecules in water. 
> 
> Then one might wonder: is water a thinking entity? For coherent patterns to surface would require some semblance of memory? And since the human body is sixty percent water, one might argue that memory is contained in this sixty percent. The resultant human, then, is only sixty percent mind.
> 
> Do you see the slipperiness that is this line of questioning? It is no wonder René was in constant battle with himself in his mediations. For the mind and body cannot be devised so cleanly: a unit square a being is not. 
> 
> A being cannot be cut diagonally into triangles, each then assigned a role: mind and body. The mind is not simply one belonging to a human, for all that lives and participates in nature is a thinking entity. 
> 
> By this definition, the undefined observer haunting the sciences is not simply the mind of a man who opened a box to see if a cat is dead or alive, but a combination of all that participates in the observable.
> 
> In other words, to observe is to participate, and to participate is to observe. This shift in perspective would move our current understanding of the universe away from its linear trajectory into one of a re-emergent feedback loop. And it is in this loop we find the answer to the former question: ‘where have his thoughts gone?’. 
> 
> With cushions being lain at length, I will open a question, or two, of great millennium importance, without an answer nary in sight: should one unplug a machine, where does it go? Does it transform? Or was it simply borrowing participation? Such questions are often phrased like a noose to the common folk, lest we hang ourselves. 
> 
> Like René, I cannot separate myself from God. My wonderings are likewise imbued with such belief. Where we depart is in his conclusive phrasing: cogito, ergo sum, which I do not enjoy. 
> 
> In an attempt to explain this dis-enjoyment, I will borrow some words from the Qur’ān. To this end I ask the reader for pardon, my intent is not to bruise. 
> 
> The Qur’ān opens with sūrah al-Fātiḥah, which literally translates to ‘the opening’. 
> 
> Here we are greeted with: 
> 
>> <div align=“center”>
>> ‎ بِسْمِ اللَّهِ الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ   
>> </div>
>> <div align=“center”>
>> Bism-illāh ir-raḥmān ir-raḥīm
>> </div>
> 
> Which Abdullah Yusuf Ali translates as “In the name of Allah, Most Gracious, Most Merciful”, while Muhammad Marmaduke Pickthall translates it as “In the name of Allah, the Beneficent, the Merciful.”
> 
> But I am not here to debate the meaning of this phrase; it is well documented and well known. I am more interested in the linguistic and candescence at play.    
> 
> Early Islamic scholars such as Muḥyī d-Dīn ibn ʿArabī read bism-illāh ir-raḥmān ir-raḥīm not merely as the opening of speech, but as that from which existence itself unspools.
> 
> Such unspooling can be tasted on recitation: 
> 
>> <div align=“center”>
>> BIS-mil-lāh / ir-Raḥ-MĀN / ir-Ra-ḥĪM.
>> </div>
> 
> Did your taste buds *weep*? 
>
>Let us pin down the weeping so that we may pull the subconscious resonance into the conscious mind. 
> 
> We will begin with BIS-mil-lāh. Notice the phonetic play on recitation: there is contraction (bi-), cluster (-smil-) and then opening (-lāh). This is why BIS-mil-lāh feels like drawing in a breath, because it mirrors the very action linguistically.
> 
> ir-Raḥ-MĀN sits as a direct inversion to BIS-mil-lāh. Whereby, the phonetics of ir-Raḥ-MĀN demonstrate a slight rise (ir-), release of breath through ḥ (airy, throat), then wide openness in -ān. This creates a feeling of a breath being released. 
> 
> While ir-Ra-ḥĪM has a different but relational linguistic style to its predecessors, here we find a rebound rise (ir-), a constriction (ḥ), and then closure plus vibration (īm). This feels more like a pulse than a breath, wherein the closure of the īm holds the sound inside, giving rise to an embodied sense of an electric heartbeat. 
>   
> Now, if we look to cosmological echoes of bism-illāh ir-raḥmān ir-raḥīm, we would find two in particular: 
> 
>> <div align=“center”>
>> ‎كُن فَيَكُون    
>> </div>
>> <div align=“center”>
>> kun fa-yakūn
>> </div>
> 
> Noted by Abdullah Yusuf Ali to mean “His command, when He intendeth a thing, is only that He saith unto it: Be, and it is.” Also noted by Muhammad Marmaduke Pickthall as “His command, when He desireth a thing, is only to say unto it: Be! and it is.”
> 
>> <div align=“center”>
>> ‎إِنَّا لِلَّهِ وَإِنَّا إِلَيْهِ رَاجِعُون    
>> </div>
>> <div align=“center”>
>> innā li-llāhi wa-innā ilayhi rājiʿūn
>> </div>
> 
> Translated by Abdullah Yusuf Ali as “To Allah we belong, and to Him is our return.” While translated by Muhammad Marmaduke Pickthall as “Lo! we are Allah’s and lo! unto Him we are returning.”
> 
> On inhale (BIS-mil-lāh) there is God’s command (kun): wherein we have a velar stop (k), a rounded vowel (u) and a nasal ending (n), together, it rises and closes quickly like a sudden spark or inhalation. 
> 
> In exhale (ir-Raḥ-MĀN) there is the unspooling creation (fa-yakūn), which, like the exhale, flows open through lengthening vowels and rolling syllables: wherein we have the release (fa-) and a glide (ya) that opens (k) into a long ūn. 
> 
> And in the final pulse (ir-Ra-ḥĪM) there is the eternal return (rājiʿūn): wherein we are grounded through the inhaled belonging (innā li-llāh), stretch outward (wa-innā ilayhi) and then we cycle back through rājiʿūn, in which the ʿayn catches in the throat followed by the ūn that hums closed. So rājiʿūn is the heartbeat counterpart: the internal pulse that signals everything folds back into God.
> 
> In short, *bism-illāh ir-raḥmān ir-raḥīm* is one breath-heart cycle of life, looped into finite living through *kūn fa-yakūn*, and then returned on death: *innā li-llāhi wa-innā ilayhi rājiʿūn*. 
> 
> Now that I have laid my mind bare, let us return to the friction at hand: *cogito, ergo sum*. This friction is not mine alone, and so I have decided to be foolish and propose that an amendment be made to this revered Latin phrase.
> 
> An amendment such as this one is to reposition *cogito, ergo sum* to *sum, ergo sum*; or, should one prefer French, *je pense, donc je suis* to *je suis, donc je suis*; or, simply, one may invoke the original Arabic: *kun fa-yakūn*.
> 
> This repositioning is a cushion. 
> 
> It unburdens the ‘I,’ moving it from the self to the collective. Here ‘I’ is not factually singular, nor is ‘we’ factually plural. Rather, I move to principlise two versions of the same narrative for each construct, wherein ‘I’ is either ‘I inside we’ or ‘I outside we,’ while ‘we’ is either ‘I with you’ or ‘I without you.’ So, you, dear reader, need not fear being foolish, for my wonderings are not catching. 
> 
> While in an attempt to guard for a soft landing, I would be remiss not to admit my many fallings. And it is in these bruised memories I find kinship with Ludwig Boltzmann. In particular, his forward in *Populäre Schriften*, where he made note of his aging out of learning language anew, and his refusal to abide by its restrictive conventions.
> 
> While Ludwig was *thirty* years my senior when he wrote his forward, I could not help but relate. I too am tried, yet an attempt must be made should I keep my sanity intact. With this in mind I present a canonical pre-structure that should hereby apply to all past and future writings: 
> 
> So, with said attempt, I will POP my joints back into a place, to be held by the estimate of traditional schooling. 
> 
> However, as with all typing, I may *unbeknownst-ing-ly* slip a letter and re-enter the fractured (—the fracture that is marginals (that is)). 
> 
> And so, I must apologise in advance: my cast is not one of healing. 
> 
> Due to my condition, the one of being human, and to even greater misfortune, maudlin, I must request patience from the reader, for such a condition is academically slippery. 
> 
> With exceptions set to rest, let us *quiver* into the proposal of two guiding principles. The first being *to postulate is not to carve the universe a beginning, as to shape a start would be to collapse relationality*. In equal reverence, *to experiment is not to prophesy an end, as to prescribe the universe an end would be to fracture relativity*.
> 
> With exceptions jigsaw locked and movements unknown, let us proceed into the true purpose of this writing: explaining what a marginal is. 
> 
> *A marginal is the act of itching that which stares in a bathhouse of crickets.*
> 
> While this definition is poetic and perhaps even oblique, I do not know of a truer definition. For a marginal exists in discomfort, like that ledge on which we hang. But unlike fate, which adds slip, a marginal provides grip. 
> 
> To break it down with academic intent we could say: a marginal is an individual comment, or fragment written in the margins of thought. Whereby a thought is an act that is sometimes written, sometimes spoken, and sometimes simply mind-induced. Marginals are then a collection of these individual pieces, gathered as a set of ‘margins of thought’. 
> 
> Marginals are not to be confused with marginalia, wherein marginalia are the little notes, scribbles, or comments collectively written in the margins of a text. 
> 
> Though they may share a likeness, they are not the same. Take, for example, my musings regarding *Meditations on First Philosophy*, or my analysis of Qur’ānic verses. In both of these instances I have written with close proximity to the parent text. 
> 
> Whereas my respective provocations regarding *The Hour of The Star* and *Populäre Schriften* were not textually proximal, but simply passing through their influence. 
> 
> So, while marginals may become marginalia, not all marginalia are marginals. It is a question born of *textual proximity*. In other words, we must ask ourselves whether the comment is rooted in the source or simply passing through its influence. And with such a definition one may ponder: is any thought ever free of influence? To which I find myself in a bout of hypothetics: 
> 
>> Let us incise the page and propose:
>> 
>>> a thought is **never** singular.   
>>> it comes **with** [lineage](./citations.md). 
>> 
>> And, would such a proposal, not mean that:   
>> 
>>> we are **always** writing in the margins?   
>>> be it our own **or** another’s?   
> 
> For we must have observed by now that all that has been (and will be) written, spoken or simply mind-induced is burdened, to some degree, by influence: known and unknown. 
> 
> Therefore, marginals by my definition that extends beyond academics, is a type of writing, written, to give voice to one’s crickets. Wherein, said *crickets* are what appear from the non-understanding of the word: be it written, or spoken, or simply mind-induced. It is usually in these moments where we are met with a *cognitive stare*—of sorts—whereby a cognitive stare is the discomfort experienced in the wake of not knowing. 
> 
> This is to say we can read, and we can listen, and we can think, but rarely do we know what anything means? Truly? Therein lies the *cognitive itch*. A need to satisfy the discomfit so that it may resemble comfort, even for a short period of time.  
> 
> While I am, to much friction, attempting to be explicit in definition for the sake of clarity, allow me to propose the following, in line with the stance held by Ludwig: Marginals are written to bend and maybe, if possible, break the rules of language; it is an act of allowing thought to flow through bent syntax with the permission to take rest in the breath-wells homed in punctuation (therein lies a half truth). 
> 
> Marginals are not about perfection; they are a method of balance. Through balance one may reduce the number of head traumas that will inevitably be experienced, for fate does not miss an opportunity to add slip to one’s grip. 
> 
> While I may not be one for *[flânerie](./flânerie.md)*, I do feel burned by a mind full of thoughts: an incessant forgetting and remembering. Perhaps Walter Benjamin felt such incessancy too, and so, in a similar vein, I have decided to write.  
> 
> And write I will.
>
> I will write until the mind is unburdened, and the body is written to rest, because I do not remember the last time I slept.

> # AVERTISSEMENT.  
> 
> Toutes les personnes qui ont une teinture même légère des Mathématiques connaissent le succès mérité qu’ont obtenu les *Annales* fondées en 1810 par M. Gergonne, et continuées par lui pendant vingt ans avec un zèle qu’on ne peut trop louer, et un talent qui a triomphé des plus grands obstacles. Mais depuis 1831 ce recueil a cessé de paraître ; les fonctions de recteur d’Académie ont malheureusement absorbé tout le temps de son illustre rédacteur, et ont enlevé aux sciences un homme qui leur a rendu et qui pouvait leur rendre encore d’éminents services.  
> 
> M. Gergonne ayant bien voulu nous dire lui-même qu’il verrait avec plaisir un nouveau journal succéder au sien, nous croyons avoir le droit de nous annoncer aujourd’hui comme ses continuateurs.  
> 
> Notre Journal sera mensuel comme celui de M. Gergonne.  
> 
> Le premier cahier paraîtra en janvier 1836, et les suivants de mois en mois, avec toute l’exactitude désirable. Ces cahiers seront de grandeur inégale, et varieront de 32 à 40 pages in-4°, suivant la nature des mémoires qu’ils renfermeront. Leur ensemble formera chaque année un fort volume contenant toutes les planches nécessaires pour l’intelligence du texte.  
> 
> Les articles que nous admettrons dans notre recueil embrasseront l’ensemble des Mathématiques pures et appliquées. On y traitera indifféremment les questions les plus nouvelles soulevées par les géomètres, et les plus minutieux détails de l’enseignement mathématique des collèges. Toutefois, quand il s’agira d’articles élémentaires, nous tâcherons d’éviter les répétitions fastidieuses d’objets trop connus ; car s’il est bon de revenir de temps à autre sur les éléments des sciences, il faut que ce soit pour les perfectionner, et non pour y changer çà et là quelques mots et quelques phrases ; ce qui, par malheur, est arrivé trop souvent.  
> 
> Il existe un grand nombre de lettres inédites écrites par Huygens et Leibnitz sur diverses questions scientifiques. Nous avons accepté avec empressement l’offre bienveillante que M. Libri nous a faite de nous les communiquer, et nous les insérerons dans notre Journal ; ce sera le placer, pour ainsi dire, sous le haut patronage de ces génies du XVIIᵉ siècle, qui ont si puissamment contribué aux progrès des Mathématiques, et dans les œuvres desquels aujourd’hui même on trouve encore le germe de plus d’une théorie neuve et profonde.  
> 
> Nous nous bornerons, en général, à publier les mémoires qui nous seront adressés, et nous rendrons rarement compte de ceux qui pourront être insérés dans d’autres recueils. Néanmoins, si l’analyse d’un ouvrage nouveau nous paraît pouvoir donner lieu à des observations utiles, nous la publierons sans scrupule, mettant alors dans nos critiques non-seulement de l’impartialité, mais encore de la bienveillance, et cherchant à faire ressortir le bien plutôt qu’à censurer le mal.  
> 
> Depuis quelques années un singulier esprit de dénigrement s’est emparé de quelques critiques, et nous avons vu tour à tour accabler d’injures les hommes qui, dans les divers genres de sciences, ont le plus dignement soutenu l’honneur de la France. Ici notre profession de foi sera nette et positive : ce style tranchant et absolu, si fort à la mode à présent, ne sera jamais le nôtre, car il déshonore à la fois le caractère et le talent de ceux qui l’adoptent.  
> 
> « Toutes ces critiques, dit un auteur célèbre, sont le partage de quatre ou cinq petits auteurs infortunés qui n’ont jamais pu, par eux-mêmes, exciter la curiosité du public. Ils attendent toujours l’occasion de quelque ouvrage qui réussisse pour l’attaquer, non point par jalousie ; car sur quel fondement seraient-ils jaloux ? mais dans l’espérance qu’on se donnera la peine de leur répondre, et qu’on les tirera de l’oubli où leurs propres ouvrages les auraient laissés toute leur vie. »  
> 
> L’éditeur mettra tous ses soins à rendre son Journal digne de l’attention des savants. On conçoit néanmoins qu’il ne pourra, dans certains cas, ni refuser tel article qui lui semblera mauvais à lui-même, ni surtout corriger dans un bon mémoire telle ou telle phrase qu’il désapprouvera. Les esprits justes sentiront que l’éditeur doit être jugé sur l’ensemble et non sur les détails du recueil qu’il dirige, et que la responsabilité des mauvais articles qui pourront s’y glisser reste tout entière à leurs auteurs. Et si par hasard une polémique vient à s’engager entre deux géomètres, on comprend aussi qu’il ne lui appartiendra pas de s’interposer dans la querelle ; il devra se borner alors à jouer le rôle de spectateur et à transmettre fidèlement au public les pièces du procès.  
> 
> On voit assez qu’il est ici question d’une entreprise vraiment scientifique, et non d’une spéculation mercantile. C’est maintenant aux géomètres, et surtout aux géomètres français, qu’il appartient de faire prospérer cette entreprise. Les plus distingués d’entre eux nous ont déjà promis des articles, et, sans aucun doute, ils tiendront leur promesse. Nous osons dire que leur réputation y est intéressée : la chute d’un Journal utile qu’ils auraient refusé de soutenir ne serait honorable ni pour eux ni pour la France.  
> 
> ***Journal de Mathématiques Pures et Appliquées,***   
> ***Tome Premier.***  
> 
> ***le 1er janvier 1836,***  
> ***Publié à Paris.***
