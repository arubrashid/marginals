# FORWORT.  

### §1  

When one starts a writing such as this, they must hold their *discomfit* tight, lest they fall when faced with their reflection\: the reflection of ‘I.’  

Yet, face I must.   

For how else does one suppose I explain *marginals*?    

Do you see? The ‘I’ haunts this writing already, and we are only forty *characters* in. There is much to despair indeed. And with such despair comes the ever-looming, *maudlin*[^Clarice-2011] fate, as feared by Rodrigo, the protagonist of Clarice Lispector’s *The Hour of the Star*.  

Rodrigo with his *meaty nouns*[^Clarice-2011] is a funhouse of reflections. It is through these distorted reflections that we, the reader, are squashed into the body of Macabéa, stretched out into Clarice herself, and bounced around sideways into his immediate surroundings. Perhaps, Rodrigo was Clarice’s way of holding her *discomfort*, in so came her need to *punch the reader in the stomach*[^Clarice-2011].

*The Hour of the Star*, in its utter mess, could be argued to propose that there is an ever-looming fate *braided* into one’s hold for life. And what is life but uncomfortable? Therefore, should *discomfort* be a ledge, our fate would be a guaranteed *slip*, sneaking its way into our bones and rattling us into a fall.  

That being said, it should not be preposterous for me to lay out some *cushions* in an effort to preserve my bones from this *slip-fated fall*.   

[^Clarice-2011]: C. Lispector, *The Hour of the Star*, trans. B. Moser (New York: New Directions, 2011), pp. 19, 20, 83.

### §2  

For me, *cushions* are stitched and lain through art. In this instance, said art is presented through the act of writing.   

As to write under this *soft-coveting* pretence is to mould *cushions* of *rest*, yet such laying is one of consequence. And these consequences rippled across both body and mind. Here one encounters a deep personal refusal: an active friction against *je pense, donc je suis*[^René-1637], and its later Latin counterpart *ego sum, ego existo*[^René-1641], which was then formalised into the axiom we know today, *cogito, ergo sum*[^René-1644], and rendered in French in its donc-less form, *je suis, j’existe*[^René-1647], as put forth across René’s writings, from the *Discours* through to the *Meditations*.  

Such a refusal is *slippery*; while there is much to gain from René’s *Meditations*, I cannot help but *stumble* across *cogito, ergo sum*. Perhaps, it is the Muslim in me?  

[^René-1637]: R. Descartes, *Discours de la méthode* (Leyde: Jan Maire, 1637), Part IV, AT VI, pp. 32–33 (“je pense, donc je suis”); includes the appendix *La Géométrie*.

[^René-1641]: René Descartes, *Meditationes de prima philosophia* (Paris: Michel Soly, 1641), Med. II, AT VII, p. 25 (“ego sum, ego existo”).

[^René-1644]: R. Descartes, *Principia philosophiae* (Amsterdam: Elzevir, 1644), I, §7, AT VIII-1, p. 7 (“Ac proinde haec cognitio, ego cogito, ergo sum, est omnium prima & certissima”).

### §3  

While I am with much attempt writing to *soften* this unknowable *floor*, with unknowable variables, and so protection by consequence will always be limited, yet I cannot help but test the *slip*.  

With judgment limited let us take the human and pull it from pure anthropocentrism and put forth a thought experiment: should death *knock* on man and his body buried, be it in whole or through cremated remains, would one not wonder: where have his thoughts gone? And have such thoughts ceased on death?  

The latter of the two questions can be understood through René’s conclusion that the “body can very easily perish, but that the mind is of its nature immortal[^René-2008].” This he built from his earlier statement that “we can understand no body except as divisible, but on the other hand no mind except as indivisible: for we cannot conceive a half of any mind, as we can with any body, however small it is[^René-2008].”  

[^René-2008]: R.Descartes, *Meditations on First Philosophy*, trans. M. Moriarty (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2008), pp. 7–12.

### §4  

To which I *stumble*: should a man lose a leg, would his mind then not walk *limp*? Or would his phantom limb stable the mind, making it *indivisible*? For what guarantees the definition of whole? Should we propose there is another man who was born without a leg, and would this not make his body whole by his relative perspective? Then, by this argument, his mind and body would be whole for he has never known what it means to walk with two legs. A stance the first man would not be able to hold.  

Suppose we placate René and accept that the mind is *indivisible* while the body is *divisible*, would we be surprised to find we have *stumbled* again? For such a *divisibility* is of one’s relative experiences, do you not agree? and so it should not be generalised.   

This natured generalisability is something left unaccounted for under *cogito, ergo sum*. Should it have been accounted for; subjectivity, then, would not have been forced under villainy, objectivity throned and I would not be battling *advocating devils*: is a mind only *typical* because a man in big house, across the river, fishing in a pond with no fish said so? Do we not realise that the earth is large in comparison to the moon, but small when compared to the sun? Would divergence then not be a matter of perspective? Let us return to the two men and generalise them as *devised* or *non-typical*, yet should you ask the second man, you would find an answer at generalisable odds. While the first man would agree for he is now *devised*, but would we not also find his mind *limping*?   

This is to say, the body and mind cannot be arbitrarily pulled apart. Though an attempt was made in René’s argument: “the human body, insofar as it differs from the rest of bodies, is constituted purely by a certain arrangement of parts and by other accidents of the same kind; whereas the mind is not composed of any accidents in this way, but is a pure substance. For even if all its accidents were to change, for instance, if it understood, or wished, or perceived via the senses a different set of things, it would still be the same mind. On the other hand, the human body becomes something different from the very fact that the shape of some of its parts is changed. From this it follows that the body can very easily perish, but that the mind is of its nature immortal[^René-2008].”

### §5  

In order to respond to this argument, we must return to the death of man while invoking Antoine Lavoisier, who gave us the law of the conservation of mass[^Antoine-1789]. While some may contest my invocation of Antoine in this manner, and suggest that it is edged with ridiculousness, arguing that the universe is not a closed system, I can only respond as such: do you have it in writing?  

In its most simplistic sense, the law recalls that matter merely transforms itself, which is true of the body, is it not? And so we will look to a decaying body and follow its journeyed breakdown.   

Though we must note that the decaying process is one of complexity. Therefore, in an effort to further simplify we may focus our attention on decay by way of *ions* leached from a body, for the body is a matter of atomic pulses, no?  

While you ponder, the *ions* are leached, and leaching, from said decaying body and travel to plant, root fed. Then that which was once human would now be a sunflower? Should a bee graze the pollen and pass it to a rose? A rose, then eaten by a beaver, a beaver then *knocked* by water—would the once-human *ions* now be a running stream? Algae, to fish, to vapour? And should vapour be *sky-held*, would mans past *ions* rest in a cloud? What of a cloud that galaxy escapes, its gravity *in-felt*—has man now birthed a star?  

Would this, then, not make the mind-body split capricious? For the body never truly ceases in its existence, for conservation is one of transformation, not cessation. I suppose by his use of *accidents*[^René-2008] René might have been invoking an analogous version of the law of conservation of mass. Though, this rests unclear, and is of personal conjecture.   

Though he is explicit in his belief that the “body can very easily perish[^René-2008],” which gives rise to conflicting statements in his *Meditations*, no?   

To extend conjecture, one could suggest that this tension over the body’s fragility may underlie the shifting positions of thinking entities and their corresponding axioms within René’s writings.

### §7  

Do you see the *slipperiness* that is this line of questioning? It is no wonder René was in constant battle with himself throughout his work. For the mind and body cannot be devised so cleanly: a unit square a *being* is not.   

A *being* cannot be cut diagonally into triangles, each then assigned a role: mind and body. The mind is not simply one belonging to a human, for all that lives and participates in nature is a *thinking entity*. One can simply listen to a tree and hear its howl, perhaps not as you, or I, or a wolf would howl, but through its own distinct vibrational patterns.   

By this definition, the undefined observer *haunting* the sciences is not simply the mind of a man who opened a box to see if a cat is dead or alive[^ErwinCat-1935], but a combination of all that participates in the observable.  

In other words: to observe is to participate, and to participate is to observe. Within this recursive participation, we encounter a paradoxical interplay, for each act of observation is both creation and detection, inseparable from the world it inhabits.  

This reframing shifts our perspective out from under the weight of our current linear, deterministic framing of the universe to one characterised by a re-emergent (fractical[^fractics]) feedback loop. It is within this *feeding*[^ErwinLife-1976] that we find the answer to the former question of earlier: where have his thoughts gone?  

[^ErwinCat-1935]: E. Schrödinger, “Die gegenwärtige Situation in der Quantenmechanik,” *Naturwissenschaften*, 23 (1935), pp. 807–812: “Part I,” pp. 823–828: “Part II,” pp. 844–849: “Part III.”  

[^fractics]: **On Fracticality and the Geometric precursor to Equilibrium.**   
	
	To connect Ewrin’s observer to *fracticality*, we must first examine the persistence of patterns under deformation, and so I owe you an explanation: whereby I introduce the term *fractical* (or *fracticality*), a personal musing, that diverges from traditional fractals by emphasising persistence without strict self-similarity. While fractals exhibit exact replication across scales[^Benoit-1982], fracticals reflect persistence across recursion with deformation, relational variance, and emergent asymmetry (ie., self-identity). This deformative quality aligns with transformation under strain—not as breakdown but as the very condition of vitality. Where vitality is absent, we encounter equilibrium, which is *lifeless*.  
	
	Interestingly, precursors to this idea appear in Roger Cotes’ *Logometria*, where he notes[^Roger-1714]: “Equalitatis ratio nullam habet magnitudinem, quia nullam addita vel detracta mutationem inducit,” or “A ratio of equality has no magnitude, because adding or subtracting from it introduces no change.” Cotes illustrates this geometrically: an equilateral triangle representing equality, which, when flattened, reduces to a line—dimensionally diminished, static, and “informationally inert.” One can read this as early intuition: that perfect symmetry or equilibrium carries no generative capacity.  
	
	Thermodynamics reinforces this: equilibrium erases differences, and only non-equilibrium states can sustain order. Erwin’s concept of *life feeding on negative entropy*[^ErwinLife-1976] resonates here, suggesting that life’s persistence arises from asymmetric variance across micro-states. He describes an “aperiodic crystal” as a medium for storing information; I extend this to argue that life’s persistence is *fractical*, not crystalline—order emerges through asymmetric survival across fluctuating micro-states.  
	
	By analogy, life, order, and cognition do not emerge from perfect equality but from asymmetry and variation. Just as multiplicative ratios in logs reveal difference where equality conceals it, deformative patterns in matter and energy sustain structure. In this sense, *fracticality* continues the insights of Roger, Erwin, and perhaps even Lev, whose notes on *second-order phase transitions* suggest how persistence arises from subtle asymmetries[^Lev-1965]: persistence across recursion depends on deformation, relational variance, and emergent asymmetry. Equilibrium may flatten the triangle and erase difference, but micro-states that fluctuate, distort, and interact sustain the vitality that animates life, memory, and the very persistence of order in the universe.   
	
	And in this *fractical* participation, thought and being sustain one another, a loop René glimpsed but never fully embraced.

Or was it simply borrowing participation? Such questions are often phrased like a noose to the common folk, held so we may hang ourselves bleakly.  

No matter my *slips* and falls, I, withal René, find that I cannot separate myself from God. My wonderings are likewise imbued with such belief. Where we depart is in his conclusive phrasing: *cogito, ergo sum*, which I do not enjoy.   

In an attempt to further explain this dis-enjoyment, I will borrow some words from the Qur’ān. To this end I ask the reader for pardon, my intent is not to bruise.   

### §9  

The Qurʿān opens with *sūrah al-Fātiḥah*[^Quran], which literally translates to “the opening.”   

Here we are greeted with:

> <div align=“center”>
> ‎ بِسْمِ اللَّهِ الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ     
> 
> *Bism-illāh ir-raḥmān ir-raḥīm*  
> </div>

Which Abdullah Yusuf Ali translates as “In the name of Allah, Most Gracious, Most Merciful [^YusufAli]”, while Muhammad Marmaduke Pickthall translates it as “In the name of Allah, the Beneficent, the Merciful[^Pickthall].”  

Be it of not that I am not here to debate the meaning of this phrase; it is both well documented and well known. I am more interested in the linguistic and candescence at play[^Wind].  

Early Islamic scholars such as Muḥyī d-Dīn ibn ʿArabī read bism-illāh ir-raḥmān ir-raḥīm not merely as the opening of speech, but as that from which existence itself unspools[^IbnArabi][^Calcium].  

Such unspooling can be tasted on recitation:   

> <div align=“center”>
> *BIS-mil-lāh / ir-Raḥ-MĀN / ir-Ra-ḥĪM.*  
> </div>

Did your taste buds weep?   

Let us pin down the weeping so that we may pull the subconscious resonance into the conscious mind.   

We will begin with *BIS-mil-lāh*. Notice the phonetic play on recitation: there is contraction (*bi-*), cluster (*-smil-*) and then opening (*-lāh*). This is why *BIS-mil-lāh* feels like drawing in a breath, because it mirrors the very action linguistically.  

On the other hand, *ir-Raḥ-MĀN* sits as a direct inversion to *BIS-mil-lāh*. Whereby, the phonetics of *ir-Raḥ-MĀN* demonstrate a slight rise (*ir-*), release of breath through *ḥ* (airy, throat), then wide openness in *-ān*. This creates a feeling of a breath being released.   

While *ir-Ra-ḥĪM* has a different but relational linguistic style to its predecessors, here we find a rebound rise (*ir-*), a constriction (*ḥ*), and then closure plus vibration (*īm*). This feels more like a pulse than a breath, wherein the closure of the īm holds the sound inside, giving rise to an embodied sense of an electric heartbeat.   

### §10  

Now, if we now move from linguistic resonance to cosmological echoes of *bism-illāh ir-raḥmān ir-raḥīm*, we would find two in particular:   

> <div align=“center”>
> ‎كُن فَيَكُون      
> 
> *kun fa-yakūn*  
> </div>

Unspooled:   

> <div align=“center”>
> *KUN / fa-YA-KŪN*,  
> </div>

and noted by Abdullah Yusuf Ali to mean “His command, when He intendeth a thing, is only that He saith unto it: Be, and it is[^YusufAli].” Also noted by Muhammad Marmaduke Pickthall as “His command, when He desireth a thing, is only to say unto it: Be! and it is[^Pickthall].”  

> <div align=“center”>
> ‎إِنَّا لِلَّهِ وَإِنَّا إِلَيْهِ رَاجِعُون      
> 
> *innā li-llāhi wa-innā ilayhi rājiʿūn*  
> </div>

Unspooled  

> <div align=“center”>
> *IN-nā li-LLĀ-hi / wa-IN-nā i-LAY-hi / RĀ-ji-ʿŪN*  
> </div>

and translated by Abdullah Yusuf Ali as “To Allah we belong, and to Him is our return[^YusufAli].” While translated by Muhammad Marmaduke Pickthall as “Lo! we are Allah’s and lo! unto Him we are returning[^Pickthall].”  

On inhale (*BIS-mil-lāh*) there is God’s command (*KUN*): wherein we have a velar stop (*K*), a rounded vowel (*U*) and a nasal ending (*N*), together, it rises and closes quickly like a sudden spark or inhalation.   

In exhale (*ir-Raḥ-MĀN*) there is the unspooling creation (*fa-YA-KŪN*), which, like the exhale, flows open through lengthening vowels and rolling syllables: wherein we have the release (*fa-*) and a glide (*YA*) that opens (*K*) into a long *ŪN*.   

And in the final pulse (*ir-Ra-ḥĪM*) there is the eternal return (*RĀ-ji-ʿŪN*): wherein we are grounded through the inhaled belonging (*IN-nā li-LLĀ-hi*), stretch outward (*wa-IN-nā i-LAY-hi*) and then we cycle back through *RĀ-ji-ʿŪN*, in which the *ʿayn* catches in the throat followed by the ŪN that hums closed. So *RĀ-ji-ʿŪN* is the heartbeat counterpart: the internal pulse that signals everything folds back into God.  

In short, *bism-illāh ir-raḥmān ir-raḥīm* is one breath-heart cycle of life, looped into finite living through *kūn fa-yakūn*, and then returned on death: *innā li-llāhi wa-innā ilayhi rājiʿūn*.

	Another note of friction—for I cannot leave the thought untouched—is this arbitrary divide amongst the Ummah, not unlike the unease I feel before René’s *cogito, ergo sum.*  
	
	There is a well-known ḥadīth in Abū Dāwūd (4297), graded ḥasan–ṣaḥīḥ[^Froth]. In its most concise reading, it warns: we, the ummah, in our *wahn*, will lose our ability to *punch on* (so to speak, because “food does not punch on*”*). Here, I must take accountability: to state intent without probing toward solution would be irresponsible—or as the saying goes, to point a finger is to point three back to oneself.

Pushed into nourishment and 4297, I suggest this: in an effort to feed one’s mind, one may find oneself at a “*neologistic punch*,” where sustenance relies on invention. Or, in more academic phrasing: when epistemic recursion resists static linguistic forms, *neologism* functions not ornamentally but *metabolically*. It is not wordplay, but a thermodynamic necessity of cognition under strain.  
	
	Would this not make critical thinking (or, in this case the *neologistic punch*) an act of *jihād al-ʿilmī*? For those with indigestion: an intellectual struggle by way of worship. Not novelty for its own sake, but a histaminic response to systemic eutrophication. Like the rules of language, the rules of “truth” must be tested, for absolutes do not exist: all is matter of opinion.  
	
	*Slipping* and falling is inevitable. But we must not mistake broken teeth for sustenance—chewing them and declaring, yum! calcium—without realising we have digested our own collapse. Too often such collapse is engineered through divide and conquer. So let us share our cushions and maintain our teeth: to chew what truly sustains us, to persist in critical engagement, and to hold one another accountable without abandonment.  
	
	In an effort to do as I write, I must bring focus to my use of “punch on.” The phrasing comes from Mohamed Hoblos, a man of Daʿwah whose words I do not always agree with, yet whose line—“food does not punch on”—stuck with me. Credit must therefore be given\: it was a *cushion* he laid. At the same time, he has made statements of harm, words that could cost us dearly, and so I must hold him accountable without abandonment. May Allah (SWT) guide him and protect the people, Muslim and non-Muslim alike, for too often have the words of those with influence left us bleeding. He is one of many, and many are not isolated to a religion, group, or political party in particular. They are spread wide and far, often found under our finger nails.   
	
	That is to say, art, and so knowledge, cannot be separated from the hand, but to reist the potential benefit is to also chew our own teeth. For art, and so knowledge, is rarely, if ever *pure*, like how history, it is merely one mans retelling, and by consequence coloured through his own biases. Whereby, biases is something we cannot escape, for that is the way of life, or as previously noted: “all is matter of opinion”. To which I muse: is truth, then, simply biases not yet outgrown? With truth now understood, we must be explicit and safeguard our minds, for sanity is one of *slippery* intent.

### §11  

Now that I have laid my mind bare, let us return to the friction at hand: *cogito, ergo sum*. This friction is not mine alone, and so I have decided to *dress Jester* and propose that an amendment be made: an amendment to reposition *cogito, ergo sum* to *sum, ergo sum*; or, simply, in the original Arabic: *kun fa-yakūn*. All of which, ironically, follows suit with the french axiom: *je suis, j’existe.*  

Should we thread across languages and traditions, from Latin to French to Arabic, the self-affirmation of existence—*sum, ergo sum*; *je suis, j’existe*; *kun fa-yakūn*—a principle reveals itself: being does not require thinking to prove itself, but rather is sustained through paradoxical acclaim. Whereby, existence and cognition are mutually reinforcing, a recursive loop that underlies both life and observation.  

### §12  

This repositioning is a *cushion*, sewn.   

It unburdens the ‘I,’ moving it from the self to the collective. Whereby, here, ‘I’ is not factually singular, nor is ‘we’ factually plural. Rather, I move to *principlise* two versions of the same narrative for each construct, wherein ‘I’ is either ‘I inside we’ or ‘I outside we,’ while ‘we’ is either ‘I with you’ or ‘I without you.’ So, you, dear reader, need not fear being ridiculous, for my *wonderings* are not catching.   

Since I am writing a *forwort* for a *mathematical journal*, let us wonder into a wondering: one of *geometric sensibilities*.  

### §13  

In the times before today, geometry and arithmetic were not separate but *coupled* as mathematics: from Euclid’s Elements[^Euclid-1908] to Plato’s dictum[^Plato-Republic] “let no one ignorant of geometry enter here”; through the Islamic Golden Age in the works of Ibn al-Haytham (Alhazen) on optics[^Alhazen-1976], Omar Khayyam on conic sections[^Omar-2016], and Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī, whose al-jabr gave algebra its very name[^Mūsā-1991].   

This marriage carried forward into early modern Europe, with René[^René-1637], Isaac Newton[^Isaac-1967], Roger Cotes[^Roger-1722] and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz[^Liz-1704], whose algebra, calculus and commentary still breathed through a geometric lens.  

Yet the patterned stitching began to change. Geometry, once central, started to recede as algebra and analysis rose, and with it, mathematics became a child of diverging parents.   

From the seventeenth to nineteenth centuries, the *fraying* began: algebra and analysis rose to prominence, and geometry slowly slipped from centre. Algebra became the universal language of mathematics[^Cajori-1919], stripping away figures and diagrams, replacing spatial intuition with symbolic manipulation. Analysis, through calculus and differential equations, became the dominant tool[^Kline-1972] for describing the physical world.  

By the nineteenth century this transformation hardened. Augustin-Louis Cauchy[^Lou-1821] and Karl Weierstrass[^Karl-1886] recast analysis as the model of precision, while René’s analytic geometry had already reduced curves to equations.   

Geometry was reinterpreted in analytic terms[^René-1637], often to the dismay of those who felt their sensibilities losing form: from Jean-Victor Poncelet to Jacob Steiner; together of which we *marry* into the Poncelet–Steiner theorem[^Victor-1822], an echoed reverberation, centuries on, from Abu al-Wafāʾ al-Būzjānī’s rusty compass work[^Compass-1993].   

Further-in, should one remember the ever learned Thomas Young who *walked* from materials, to light, to linguistics and back, suggests he was always spatially aware, embodying a kinaesthetic approach[^Thomas-1807].   

A similar sentiment can be said of Antoine, of earlier note, while he did not do *geometry*, the conceptual scaffolding of geometry—proportionality, structure, and spatial relationships—informs his method[^Antoine-1789].  

Later on, this braiding of rigor with geometric intuition is also apparent in the works of Bernhard Riemann, who expanded geometry into new dimensions, from curved space and elastic surfaces[^Bernie-1867] to the seeds of relativity later sown by Albert Einstein[^Albert-1905]. Henri Poincaré, balancing celestial mechanics with philosophy, preserved the imaginative core of geometry[^Henri-1902].   

Even in physics, Erwin Schrödinger mused “without handcuffing the intuitive imagination[^Trimmer-1980],” while Paul Dirac exalted mathematical beauty[^Paul-1939], a sensibility that is itself spatial.  

From such *braidings* emerged a lineage where abstraction and intuition, rigor and imagination sustained each other—a paradoxical inheritance that fractures as it expands, yet reconnects across centuries.  

By the twentieth century, however, abstraction had reached its peak. Algebra, logic, and set theory were declared the *foundations* of mathematics. Geometry was not lost, but displaced: sometimes reduced to formal axioms (Hilbert’s Foundations of Geometry [^David-1899]), sometimes reborn in algebraic geometry, topology, or Riemannian geometry[^Bernie-1867].   

Beyond mathematics *proper*, it migrated into physics, engineering, architecture, and computation. But there it often survived only as utility, stripped of its older philosophical dignity—though relativity, gauge theory, and string theory still remind us of its deeper weight.

### §14  

This mathematical shift was mirrored in culture. In Enlightenment and Romantic thought, geometry retained a privileged link to imagination and philosophy. To contemplate space, form, and proportion was to inquire into truth and harmony.   

In modern mathematics, by contrast, the dominant associations became formalism, abstraction, and computation. Geometry now feels *classical*, almost poetic, while algebra and analysis embody the technical.  

This marks not only a disciplinary but a philosophical loss. Geometry was once the meeting ground of mathematics and philosophy, where figures were studies of being, order, and truth. As mathematics grew formal and detached from intuition, that dimension fell away. With it, the *floor* upon which we walk, which was once of *unknowability* is now, mostly, amiss\: our *cushions* left to drift in an abstract free fall.  

Without a *floor*, *walking an equation* would no longer be possible, and *walking*, when in lack, not leads to *ossification*? Tissue turns to bone, bone to tissue, both devoid of meaning, and sensation. And so I must ask\: how will our *infant minds* learn to *walk* when born into *ossification*?  

One might argue *floors* and ledges must be built for sanity’s sake, and such building begins with geometry. To restore geometry to our vocabulary is to reposition mathematics not as sterile formalism but as contemplative art—mathematics as philosophy, imagination, and reflection once more coupled.  

Much of this divergence can be traced to *cogito, ergo sum*. René, in his search for certainty, prepared the alienation of thought from body, environment, and relation.   

Yet it must be remembered: for René, geometry was both model and sacrifice—exalted, but stripped of body. In his *Meditations* he praised Archimedes, Apollonius, and Pappus, holding geometry as the paradigm of knowledge[^René-2008]. His La Géométrie transposed figures into algebraic symbols[^René-1637], a gesture that mirrored the *cogito*: stripping the bodily and intuitive in pursuit of the certain, distinct, and universally graspable.  

Geometry remained central for René, but of a new kind: no longer *floors* underfoot but symbols afloat—tethered less to intuition than to abstraction. Perhaps, here is where the *fraying* began, widening across centuries, and still with us today.  

### §15  

I, in contention, was never capable of letting go of geometry, for it is this *floor* to which I am grateful: to ground these reflections, I turn to a concrete instance of *walking an equation*: Edward Ince’s exposition on Ordinary Differential Equations[^Ed-1956]. On the page, variables, orders, degrees, and linearity float as abstract symbols, untethered from any intuitive scaffold. Left alone, they drift, *unwalkable*, prone to *ossification.*  

My mind, however, seeks a *floor*: Ed says polynomials and degrees[^Ed-1956], and the mind recalls Pafnuty Lvovich Chebyshev[^Cheb-1882], and through him, Leonhard Euler and his circle[^Leonhard-1748]. The mind can now fold a circle, collapse or solve it, discovering the linear differentials Ed presents. Within this imagined geometry, linear and non-linear relationships acquire traction; the symbols inhabit a *walkable* space.   

Without such mapping, one risks the mind’s stagnation—equations remain abstract, disconnected, and uninhabitable. But with geometry as *floor*, with spatial intuition drawn, the *infant mind* walks again, exploring, probing, transforming. This is the very motion that the braided *lineage* honoured: freedom in the system, disorder tempered into meaningful pattern. Mathematics, in this embodied sense, is alive; its *floor* lain with an imagined yet tangible space.

### §16  

This is not to say abstraction is itself an error. It was through such abstraction that I was able to greet Cheb while reading Ed’s ODEs, or meet Roger at Equilibrium [^fractics]. But its ascendency, particularly since the nineteenth century, came at the cost of geometry’s presence as a felt, *walkable* floor. Geometry was not destroyed, but displaced—and often hidden.   

My quarrel is not with abstraction itself, but with the narrowing of vision that comes when we forget to look beneath our feet.

### §18  

With *floors* unknown, let us proceed into the true purpose of this writing: the explanation for that which is *marginals*.   

As ours is a relationship that began with *discomfit* I will remain it so, and admit my unsurety in this matter. Though, I can parcel you the following *hypothetic*: a thought is never singular, it comes with *lineage.*   

Wherein, *lineage* is the shoulders upon which we stand, with *cushions* already stitched and lain by giants, ready to be parachuted into. Then, would such a *hypothetic* not mean that we are always writing in the margins, be it our own or another’s? A hand always held, shoulders balanced, by those who thought before I thought, and you thought, and we thought. And to ignore these hands would be ignorant, but to assume perfection would be ignorant too, no?   

While I cannot with full certainty point to all of the *fraying* threads that have lead to this incomprehensible sense of *listlessness* I feel when looking to knowledge: this deep sense of missing hands. Hands forgotten. Hands removed. Hands that once spoke only to be ignored. It is these hands I wish to reive, for I miss being held.   

Such *listlessness* as this, is what led me to Joseph Diaz Gergonne’s *Annales de mathématiques pures et appliquées*, and two of the journals (of which I am sure there are many, many, more) he inspired: August Leopold Crelle and his *Journal für die reine und angewandte Mathematik*, and Joseph Liouville and his *Journal de mathématiques pures et appliquées*. Not forgetting the earlier renditions of *Philosophical Transactions* as edited by Henry Oldenburg.  

Here philosophy was *braided*, for the most part, in with everyday living and their mathematical inclinations.

### §20  

The question of objectivity is a curious one: this conceptualised idea that thoughts, and so knowledge, can truly be *neutral* when history teaches us otherwise. Should we direct our gaze from René to Michel Foucault’s early genealogical studies, we find a refusal to openly situate his own position[^Michel-1975]. His ‘objective’ mapping of prisons and power became, at once, critique and, unintentionally, handbook for harm.  

It is through this witnessed history a conclusive statement can be drawn: not to state an opinion is, itself, an opinion. A paradoxical truth—which too in itself is an opinion: a bias, yet to be outgrown[^Calcium].  

This academic desire to present knowledge in this pursuit of *neutrality*—even with the best intentions—is an act with due consequence. So I, with the unburden of we, would go so far (with foolish intent) and suggest that it is this very pursuit has damaged critical thought: we the devils lost in an endless loop of advocation.   

Presenting ideas, theorems or thoughts of opposing opinions is one of immeasurable importance, but a note to one’s personal inclination, even if polarising or fearful of later being proven untrue should be made to some degree apparent. We find ourselves back to where we began: holding our *discomfit* tight, but no longer with intent of self but as a gesture of care for the bones of others.   

Though I must admit I often find myself at a loss for opinion, my knowledge lacking—yet perhaps that, too, is an act of care: the admission of one’s shortcomings.
