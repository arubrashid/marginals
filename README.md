> *In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. I, Arub Rashid, daughter of Adil Rashid and Shagufta Tausf Rashid, born to the care of my grandparents Abdur Rashid and Hamida Begum Rashid, and Taj Muhammad and Soraya Begum, and held accountable to the generations of shoulders upon which I herein stand, have composed these marginals for reasons unbeknownst to myself, hoping they may benefit the sincere seeker of knowledge. May Allah forgive my errors and guide my pen toward truth.* 
> 
> # FORWORT.
> 
> When one starts a writing such as this, they must hold their *discomfit* tight, lest they fall when faced with their reflection: the reflection of ‘I.’
> 
> Yet, face I must. 
> 
> For how else does one suppose I explain *marginals*?  
> 
> Do you see? The ‘I’ haunts this writing already, and we are only forty characters in. There is much to despair indeed. And with such despair comes the ever-looming, maudlin fate, as feared by Rodrigo, the protagonist of Clarice Lispector’s *The Hour of the Star* (*).
> 
> Rodrigo with his *meaty* nouns is a funhouse of reflections.  It is through these distorted reflections that we, the reader, are squashed into the body of Macabéa, stretched out into Clarice herself, and bounced around sideways into his immediate surroundings. Perhaps, Rodrigo was Clarice’s way of holding her discomfort (tight) and ‘*punching the reader in the stomach*.’
> 
> The *Hour of the Star*, in its utter mess, could be argued to propose that there is an ever-looming fate braided into one’s hold for life. And what is life but uncomfortable? Therefore, should discomfort be a ledge, our fate would be a guaranteed *slip*, sneaking its way into our bones and rattling us into a fall.
> 
> That being said, it should not be preposterous for me to lay out some *cushions* in an effort to preserve my bones from this *slip-fated fall*. 
> 
> For me, *cushions* are stitched and lain through art. In this instance, said art is presented through the act of writing. As to write under this soft-coveting pretence is to mould cushions of hope, yet such laying is one of consequence. And these consequences rippled across both body and mind. Here one encounters a deep personal refusal: an active friction against *je pense, donc je suis*, and its later Latin counterpart *cogito, ergo sum* as put forth by René Descartes in his *Meditations* (*). 
> 
> Such a refusal is *slippery*, while there is much to gain from René’s *Mediations*, I cannot help but *stumble* across *cogito, ergo sum*. Perhaps, it is the Muslim in me?
> 
> While I am with much attempt shaping a soft landing which is only half protective as it currently stands, I cannot help but test the *slip*. So, with judgment no better let us take the human,  pull it from pure anthropocentrism and put forth a thought experiment: should death *knock* on man and his body buried, be it in whole or through cremated remains, one might *wonder*: *where have his thoughts gone?* And *have such thoughts cease on death?*
> 
> The latter of the two questions can be understood through René’s conclusion that the “body can very easily perish, but that the mind is of its nature immortal.” This he built from his earlier statement that “we can understand no body except as divisible, but on the other hand no mind except as indivisible: for we cannot conceive a half of any mind, as we can with any body, however small it is.”
> 
> To which I *stumble*: should a man lose a leg, would his mind then not *walk limp*? Or would his *phantom limb* stable the mind, making it indivisible? For what guarantees the definition of whole? Let us propose there is another man who was born without a leg, and would this not make his body whole by his relative perspective? Then, by this argument, his mind and body would be whole for he has never known what it means to walk with two legs. A stance the first man would not be able to hold. 
> 
> Suppose we placate René and accept that the mind is indivisible while the body is divisible, would we be surprised to find we have *stumbled* again? For such a divisibility is of one’s relative experiences, is it not? and so it should not be generalised. This natured generalisability is something left unaccounted for under *cogito, ergo sum*. Should it have been accounted for; subjectivity, then, would not have been forced under villainy, objectivity throned and I would not be battling *advocating devils*: is a mind only typical because a man in big house, across the river, fishing in a pond with no fish said so? Do we not realise that the earth is large in comparison to the moon, but small when compared to the sun? Would divergence then not be a matter of perspective? Let us return to the two men and generalise them as devised or non-typical,  yet should you ask the second man, you would find an answer at generalisable odds. While the first man would agree for he is now devised, but would we not also find his mind *limping*? 
> 
> This is to say, the body and mind cannot be arbitrarily pulled apart. Though an attempt was made in René’s argument: “the human body, insofar as it differs from the rest of bodies, is constituted purely by a certain arrangement of parts and by other accidents of the same kind; whereas the mind is not composed of any accidents in this way, but is a pure substance. For even if all its accidents were to change, for instance, if it understood, or wished, or perceived via the senses a different set of things, it would still be the same mind. On the other hand, the human body becomes something different from the very fact that the shape of some of its parts is changed. From this it follows that the body can very easily perish, but that the mind is of its nature immortal.” 
> 
> In order to respond to this argument, we must first return the death of man while invoking Antoine Lavoisier (for stability), who gave us the law of the conservation of mass (*). While some may contest my invocation of Antoine in this manner, and suggest that it is edged with ridiculousness, arguing that the universe is not a closed system, I can only respond as such: do you have it in writing?
> 
> In its most simplistic sense, the law recalls that nature merely transforms itself, which is also true of the body, no? So let us look to a decaying body and follow its journeyed breakdown. 
> 
> Though we must note that decay is a complex process. Therefore, in an effort to further simplify we may focus our attention on decay by way of *ions* leached from a body, for the body is a matter of atomic pulses, or no? 
> 
> While you ponder, the *ions* are leached, and leaching, from said decaying body and travel to a plant, root fed. Then that which was once human would now be a sunflower? Should a bee graze the pollen and pass it to a rose? A rose, then eaten by a beaver, a beaver then *knocked* by water—would the *once-human ions* now be a running stream? Algae to fish to vapour? And should vapour be *sky-held*, would mans *past ions* rest in a cloud? What of a cloud that galaxy *escapes*, its *gravity in-felt*—has man now birthed a star?
> 
> Would this then not make the mind-body split capricious? For the body never truly ceases in its existence, for conservation is one of transformation, not of destruction nor creation. I suppose by his use of *accidents* René might have been invoking the law of the conservation of mass. This rests unclear. 
> 
> Though he is explicit in his belief that the “body can very easily perish,” which gives rise to conflicting statements in his *Meditations*, no? 
> 
> Let us breathe life back into man and make note that the body is said to be approximately sixty percent water. While, skirting speculation, I will point to Lev Landau whose experiments demonstrated that water exhibits a persistence of structure in flow (*). Therein, he noted that coherent patterns were able to endure, however briefly, in the constant rearrangement of these flowing molecules in water. 
> 
> Then one might wonder: is water a thinking entity? For coherent patterns to surface, and re-surface, would require some semblance of memory? And since the human body is sixty percent water, one might argue that memory is contained in this sixty percent. The resultant human, then, is only sixty percent mind.
> 
> Do you see the *slipperiness* that is this line of questioning? It is no wonder René was in constant battle with himself in his *Meditations*. For the mind and body cannot be devised so cleanly: a unit square a being is not. 
> 
> A *being* cannot be cut diagonally into triangles, each then assigned a role: mind and body. The mind is not simply one belonging to a human, for all that lives and participates in nature is a thinking entity. One can simply listen to a tree and hear is howl, perhaps not as you, or I, or a wolf would howl, but through its own distinct vibrational patterns. 
> 
> By this definition, the undefined observer haunting the sciences is not simply the mind of a man who opened a box to see if a cat is dead or alive (*), but a combination of all that participates in the observable.
> 
> In other words, to observe is to participate, and to participate is to observe. This shift in perspective would move our current understanding of the universe away from its linear trajectory into one of a re-emergent (micro-static?) feedback loop. And it is in this loop we find the answer to the former question: *where have his thoughts gone?*
> 
> With *cushions* lain at length, I will open a question, or two, of great millennium importance, without an answer nary in sight: *should one unplug a machine, where does it go?* *Does it transform?* *Or was it simply borrowing participation?* Such questions are often phrased like a noose to the common folk, lest we hang ourselves. 
> 
> No matter my *slips* and falls, I, withal René, find that I cannot separate myself from God. My wonderings are likewise imbued with such belief. Where we depart is in his conclusive phrasing: *cogito, ergo sum*, which I do not enjoy. 
> 
> In an attempt to further explain this dis-enjoyment, I will borrow some words from the *Qur’ān*. To this end I ask the reader for pardon, my intent is not to bruise. 
> 
> The *Qurʿān* opens with *sūrah al-Fātiḥah* (*), which literally translates to “the opening.” 
> 
> Here we are greeted with: 
> 
>> بِسْمِ اللَّهِ الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ   
>> *Bism-illāh ir-raḥmān ir-raḥīm*
> 
> Which Abdullah Yusuf Ali translates as “In the name of Allah, Most Gracious, Most Merciful”, while Muhammad Marmaduke Pickthall translates it as “In the name of Allah, the Beneficent, the Merciful.”
> 
> Be it of not that I am not here to debate the meaning of this phrase; it is both well documented and well known. I am more interested in the linguistic and candescence at play.    
> 
> Early Islamic scholars such as Muḥyī d-Dīn ibn ʿArabī read *bism-illāh ir-raḥmān ir-raḥīm* not merely as the opening of speech, but as that from which existence itself unspools (*).
> 
> Such unspooling can be tasted on recitation: 
> 
>> *BIS-mil-lāh / ir-Raḥ-MĀN / ir-Ra-ḥĪM.*
> 
> Did your taste buds weep? 
> 
> Let us pin down the weeping so that we may pull the subconscious resonance into the conscious mind. 
> 
> We will begin with *BIS-mil-lāh*. Notice the phonetic play on recitation: there is contraction (*bi-*), cluster (*-smil-*) and then opening (*-lāh*). This is why *BIS-mil-lāh* feels like drawing in a breath, because it mirrors the very action linguistically.
> 
> On the other hand, *ir-Raḥ-MĀN* sits as a direct inversion to *BIS-mil-lāh*. Whereby, the phonetics of *ir-Raḥ-MĀN* demonstrate a slight rise (*ir-*), release of breath through *ḥ* (airy, throat), then wide openness in *-ān*. This creates a feeling of a breath being released. 
> 
> While *ir-Ra-ḥĪM* has a different but relational linguistic style to its predecessors, here we find a rebound rise (*ir-*), a constriction (*ḥ*), and then closure plus vibration (*īm*). This feels more like a pulse than a breath, wherein the closure of the *īm* holds the sound inside, giving rise to an embodied sense of an electric heartbeat. 
> 
> Now, if we look to cosmological echoes of *bism-illāh ir-raḥmān ir-raḥīm*, we would find two in particular: 
> 
>> ‎كُن فَيَكُون    
>> *kun fa-yakūn*
> 
> Unspooled: 
> 
>> *KUN / fa-YA-KŪN,*
> 
> and noted by Abdullah Yusuf Ali to mean “His command, when He intendeth a thing, is only that He saith unto it: Be, and it is.” Also noted by Muhammad Marmaduke Pickthall as “His command, when He desireth a thing, is only to say unto it: Be! and it is.”
> 
>> ‎إِنَّا لِلَّهِ وَإِنَّا إِلَيْهِ رَاجِعُون    
>> *innā li-llāhi wa-innā ilayhi rājiʿūn*
> 
> Unspooled
> 
>> *IN-nā li-LLĀ-hi / wa-IN-nā i-LAY-hi / RĀ-ji-ʿŪN*
> 
> and translated by Abdullah Yusuf Ali as “To Allah we belong, and to Him is our return.” While translated by Muhammad Marmaduke Pickthall as “Lo! we are Allah’s and lo! unto Him we are returning.”
> 
> On inhale (*BIS-mil-lāh*) there is God’s command (*KUN*): wherein we have a velar stop (*K*), a rounded vowel (*U*) and a nasal ending (*N*), together, it rises and closes quickly like a sudden spark or inhalation. 
> 
> In exhale (*ir-Raḥ-MĀN*) there is the unspooling creation (*fa-YA-KŪN*), which, like the exhale, flows open through lengthening vowels and rolling syllables: wherein we have the release (*fa-*) and a glide (*YA*) that opens (*K*) into a long ŪN. 
> 
> And in the final pulse (*ir-Ra-ḥĪM*) there is the eternal return (*RĀ-ji-ʿŪN*): wherein we are grounded through the inhaled belonging (*IN-nā li-LLĀ-hi*), stretch outward (*wa-IN-nā i-LAY-hi*) and then we cycle back through *RĀ-ji-ʿŪN*, in which the *ʿayn* catches in the throat followed by the *ŪN* that hums closed. So *RĀ-ji-ʿŪN* is the heartbeat counterpart: the internal pulse that signals everything folds back into God.
> 
> In short, *bism-illāh ir-raḥmān ir-raḥīm* is one breath-heart cycle of life, looped into finite living through *kūn fa-yakūn*, and then returned on death: *innā li-llāhi wa-innā ilayhi rājiʿūn*. 
> 
> Now that I have laid my mind bare, let us return to the friction at hand: *cogito, ergo sum*. This friction is not mine alone, and so I have decided to be *foolish* and propose that an amendment be made to this revered Latin phrase.
> 
> An amendment such as this one is to reposition *cogito, ergo sum* to *sum, ergo sum*; or, should one prefer French, *je pense, donc je suis* to *je suis, donc je suis*; or, simply, one may invoke the original Arabic: *kun fa-yakūn*.
> 
> This repositioning is a *cushion*. 
> 
> It unburdens the ‘I,’ moving it from the self to the collective. Here ‘I’ is not factually singular, nor is ‘we’ factually plural. Rather, I move to *principlise* two versions of the same narrative for each construct, wherein ‘I’ is either ‘I inside we’ or ‘I outside we,’ while ‘we’ is either ‘I with you’ or ‘I without you.’ So, you, dear reader, need not fear being *foolish*, for my *wonderings* are not catching. 
> 
> It is through these *wonderings* I often find myself troubled, of current note is my *geometric sensibilities*.
> 
> In earlier times, geometry and arithmetic were not separate but coupled as mathematics: from Euclid’s Elements (*) to Plato’s dictum “let no one ignorant of geometry enter here” (*); through the Islamic Golden Age in the works of Ibn al-Haytham (Alhazen) on optics (*), Omar Khayyam on conic sections (*), and Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī, whose al-jabr gave algebra its very name (*). This marriage carried forward into early modern Europe, with René, Isaac Newton (*), Roger Cotes (*) and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (*), whose algebra, calculus and commentary still breathed through a geometric lens.
> 
> But somewhere along this path, *mathematics* became a child of diverging parents. From the seventeenth to nineteenth centuries, the *fraying* began: algebra and analysis rose to prominence, and geometry slowly *slipped* from centre. Algebra became the universal language of mathematics, stripping away figures and diagrams, replacing spatial intuition with symbolic manipulation. Analysis, through calculus and differential equations, became the dominant tool for describing the physical world.
> 
> By the nineteenth century this transformation hardened. Augustin-Louis Cauchy (*) and Karl Weierstrass (*) recast analysis as the model of precision, while René’s analytic geometry (*) had already reduced curves to equations. Geometry was reinterpreted in analytic terms, often to the dismay of those who felt their sensibilities losing form.
> 
> Yet many still *braided* rigor with geometric intuition. Bernhard Riemann expanded geometry into new dimensions (*), from curved space and elastic surfaces to the seeds of relativity later sown by Einstein (*). Henri Poincaré, balancing celestial mechanics with philosophy, preserved the imaginative core of geometry (*). Even in physics, Erwin Schrödinger mused through “without handcuffing the intuitive imagination” (*), while Paul Dirac exalted mathematical beauty (*), a sensibility that is itself spatial.
> 
> From such *braidings* emerged a lineage where abstraction and intuition, rigor and imagination sustained each other—a paradoxical inheritance that fractures as it expands, yet reconnects across centuries.
> 
> By the twentieth century, abstraction had reached its peak. Algebra, logic, and set theory were declared the *foundations* of mathematics. Geometry was not lost, but displaced: sometimes reduced to formal axioms (Hilbert’s Foundations of Geometry (*)), sometimes reborn in algebraic geometry, topology, or Riemannian geometry. Beyond mathematics *proper*, it migrated into physics, engineering, architecture, and computation. But there it often survived only as utility, stripped of its older philosophical dignity—though relativity, gauge theory, and string theory still reminded us of its deeper weight.
> 
> This mathematical shift was mirrored in culture. In Enlightenment and Romantic thought, geometry retained a privileged link to imagination and philosophy (*). To contemplate space, form, and proportion was to inquire into truth and harmony. In modern mathematics, by contrast, the dominant associations became formalism, abstraction, and computation. Geometry now feels classical, almost poetic, while algebra and analysis embody the technical.
> 
> This marks not only a disciplinary but a philosophical loss. *Geometry* was once the meeting ground of mathematics and philosophy, where figures were studies of being, order, and truth. As mathematics grew formal and detached from intuition, that dimension fell away. With it, the floor upon which we walk through, to the side of and around formulas: our cushions left to drift in an abstract free fall.
> 
> Walking when in lack leads to *ossification*. Tissue turns to bone, bone to tissue, both devoid of meaning and sensation. And so I must ask: *how will our infant minds learn to walk when born into ossification?*
> 
> One might argue floors and ledges must be built for sanity’s sake, and such building begins with geometry. To restore geometry to our vocabulary is to reposition mathematics not as sterile formalism but as contemplative art—mathematics as philosophy, imagination, and reflection once more coupled.
> 
> Much of this divergence can be traced to *cogito, ergo sum*. René, in his search for certainty, prepared the alienation of thought from body, environment, and relation. Yet it must be remembered: for René, geometry was both model and sacrifice — exalted, but stripped of body. In his *Meditations* he praised Archimedes, Apollonius, and Pappus, holding geometry as the paradigm of knowledge (*). His *La Géométrie* transposed figures into algebraic symbols (*), a gesture that mirrored the *cogito*: stripping the bodily and intuitive in pursuit of the certain, distinct, and universally graspable.
> 
> Geometry remained central for René, but of a new kind: no longer cushions underfoot but symbols afloat—tethered less to intuition than to abstraction. Perhaps, here is where the *fraying* begin, widening across centuries, and still with us today.
> 
> I in contention was never capable of letting go of geometry, for it is this floor to which I am grateful: to ground these reflections, I turn to a concrete instance: Edward Ince’s exposition on *Ordinary Differential Equations* (*). On the page, variables, orders, degrees, and linearity float as abstract symbols, untethered from any intuitive scaffold. Left alone, they drift, unwalkable, prone to ossification.
> 
> My mind, however, seeks a floor: Ed says polynomials and degrees, and the mind recalls Pafnuty Lvovich Chebyshev (*), and through him, Leonhard Euler and his circle (*). The mind can now fold a circle, collapse or solve it, discovering the linear differentials Ed presents. Within this imagined geometry, linear and non-linear relationships acquire traction; the symbols inhabit a walkable space. 
> 
> Without such mapping, one risks the mind’s stagnation—equations remain abstract, disconnected, and uninhabitable. But with geometry as floor, with spatial intuition drawn, the infant mind walks again, exploring, probing, transforming. This is the very motion that the *braided* lineage honoured: freedom in the system, disorder tempered into meaningful pattern. Mathematics, in this embodied sense, is alive; its floor lain with an imagined yet tangible space. 
> 
> A space from within which we can *slip* and fall, which brings us back to the *cushions* and my attempt to guard for a soft landing. Though I would be remiss not to admit my many, many, *fallings*. For it was in these broken bones that I found kinship with Ludwig Boltzmann. In particular, his *forwort* in *Populäre Schriften* (*), where he made note of his aging out of learning language anew, and his refusal to abide by its restrictive conventions.
> 
> While Ludwig may have been thirty years my senior upon the publication of Populäre Schriften, I could not help but relate. I too am tried, yet an attempt must be made should I wish to keep my sanity intact. With this in mind I present a canonical *pre-structure* that should hereby apply to all past and future writings: 
> 
> So, with said attempt, I will POP my joints back into a place, to be held by the estimate of traditional schooling. However, as with all typing, I may *unbeknownst-ing-ly* slip a letter and re-enter the fractured (—the fracture that is marginals (that is)).  And so, I must apologise in advance: my cast is not one of healing. 
> 
> Due to my condition, the one of being human, and to even greater misfortune, *maudlin*, I must request patience from the reader, for such a condition is academically *slippery*. 
> 
> With exceptions set to rest, let us *quiver* into the proposal of two guiding principles. The first being *to postulate is not to carve the universe a beginning, as to shape a start would be to collapse relationality*. In equal reverence, *to experiment is not to prophesy an end, as to prescribe the universe an end would be to fracture relativity*.
> 
> With exceptions jigsaw locked and movements unknown, let us proceed into the true purpose of this writing: the explanation for that which is *marginals*. 
> 
> As ours is a relationship that began with *discomfit* I will remain it so and admit my unsurety in this matter. Though, I can parcel you the following *hypothetic*: a thought is never singular, it comes with [lineage](./CITATIONS.md)—a lineage of *cushions* already stitched and ready to be parachuted into. And, then, would such a *hypothetic* not mean that we are always writing in the margins, be it our own or another’s? A hand always held by those who thought before I thought, or you thought. To ignore these hands would be ignorant, but to assume perfection would be ignorant too, no? 
> 
> While I cannot with full certainty point to all of the *fraying* threads that have lead to this incomprehensible sense of *listlessness* felt when looking to knowledge: a deep sense of missing hands. Hands forgotten. Hands removed. Hands that once spoke only to be ignored. It is these hands I wish to reive, for I miss being held. 
> 
> It was this *listlessness* that led me to Joseph Diaz Gergonne’s *Annales de mathématiques pures et appliquées* (*), and two of the journals (of which I am sure there are many, many, more) he inspired: August Leopold Crelle and his *Journal für die reine und angewandte Mathematik* (*), and Joseph Liouville and his *Journal de mathématiques pures et appliquées* (*). Not forgetting the earlier renditions of *Philosophical Transactions* as edited by Henry Oldenburg (*).
> 
> Here philosophy was braided, for the most part, in with everyday living and their mathematical inclinations. Though, as *cogito, ergo sum* settled deeper into the bones of society, knowledge splinted into siloed knowledge pots. 
> 
> Herein lies my attempt to not simply *braid*, but go a step further and resituate art back into the hand of the mathematician, and mathematics back into the brush of a poet. A task with heft, hence my need for a cushioned softening for I am no mathematician nor am I poet. I just am a person writing—of which I am unsure.
> 
> And so, with *discomfit*, rattling bones and a *slippery* fate I present: 
> 
>> *Propo-facé de mathématiques pures et appliquées;*
>> *Forwort der reinen und angewandten Mathematik;* 
>> *Worforth of Pure and Applied Mathematics;*
>> *Vale Prae ad Matheseos Puram et Applicadam;*
>> 	*راجعن إلى الرياضيات البحتة والتطبيقية*
>> . . . 
> 
> A reviving of the personal–philosophical voice of early mathematical journals is therefore more than an antiquarian gesture. It is, in a sense, a revival of geometry, and so thought itself—not merely as technical disciplines, but as the archetype of mathematical reflection. In this lineage one finds Plato, Alhazen, René, Diaz, and others who treated mathematics as philosophy expressed in figures rather than as mere symbol-shuffling.
> 
> But in contest mine is one of an ever-growing, error embracing, front matter that slips deliberately across languages: bent forewards and all, each honouring the memory of Ludwig:
> 
>> *Den Manen Boltzmann* 
>> *des uniibertroffenen Meisters der naturwahren Schilderung* 
>> *echter, aus tiefstem Herzen kommender Begeisterung* 
>> *gewidmet* 
>> *hundert Jahre nach* 
>> *dessen Eingang in die Unsterblichkeit* 
> 
> All the while carrying their own historical, cultural, and intellectual resonance, reflecting the lineage and context of the many, many, artisans and their hands spread global. 
> 
> A front matter that is a simple humble, and likely an insufficient, attempt at recognising that knowledge is rarely universal in form, only in structure. 
> 
> And to borrow *flânerie* (*) in a what is a perhaps a selfish attempt to experience world travel without having to face the beauty of fresh air, for in recent times beauty suffocates and I wish to breathe long enough to complete this break, so that I may reset our bones anew. 
> 
> I shall without remorse borrow opening words from my predecessors: Diaz, Leo and Joseph. Their introductory commentary holds true, though I depart in one matter: objectivity. 
> 
> The question of objectivity is a curious one: this conceptualised idea that thoughts, and so knowledge, can truly be neutral when history teaches us otherwise. Should we direct our gaze from René to Michel Foucault’s early genealogical studies, we find a refusal to openly situate his own position (*). His ‘objective’ mapping of prisons and power became, at once, critique and, unintentionally, handbook for harm.
> 
> It is through this witnessed history a conclusive statement can be drawn: not to state an opinion is, itself, an opinion. A paradoxical truth—which too in itself is an opinion.
> 
> This academic desire to present knowledge in this pursuit of neutrality—even with the best intentions—is an act with due consequence. So I, with the unburden of we, would go so far (with *foolish* intent) and suggest that it is this very pursuit has damaged critical thought: we the devils lost in an endless loop of advocation. 
> 
> Presenting ideas, theorems or thoughts of opposing opinions is one of immeasurable importance, but a note to one’s personal inclination, even if polarising or fearful of later being proven untrue should be made to some degree apparent. We find ourselves back to where we began: holding our *discomfit* tight, but no longer with intent of self but as a gesture of care for the bones of others. 
> 
> Though I must admit I often find myself at a loss for opinion, my knowledge lacking—yet perhaps that, too, is an act of care: the admission of one’s shortcomings.
> 
> With care, 
> Arub.
